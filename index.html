Who?

My name is Tex and I'm from London. I started Richard Moonstreet in 2019 as I began my journey into ceramics. I try to avoid using my own name for new projects in case they go horribly wrong and it becomes your legacy, so Richard was my escape. My art name, or my 'Nom de Pot'. Thankfully things haven't gone that horribly wrong...yet. The name comes from a road in Crewe called Richard Moon Street (named apparently after a jolly chap who ran the London Northwestern Railway in the 1800's). Our Google Home device randomly gave us directions to this street, unprovoked, at 2am one morning, so it was committed to memory, more through irritation than interest.

Even though the name was plucked out the mind of a digital assistant, I realised soon that the more I learnt about ceramic art and the more I started throwing and building my own style, the more the name started to suit what I was making. Instead of a random scrawl or stamp in the base of a vessel, which differentiated my bisqueware from other artists in the studio I share, it became a ceramic identity. Now I look at Richard Moonstreet and I see a unique but still familiar style, I see twists on traditional forms and shapes which are often a bit erratic. I see both bold vibrant colours of a Raku fire but also muted earthy tones of an unglazed body. But most of all I just see something fun and different. I still predominatly throw for fun and to distract from the day job, but I hope some of the things I've created spark some joy in you!

And if they spark joy, you can buy them in the online shop! Because clay is expensive!

Seasonal

I really enjoyed throwing these little Ceramic Christmas trees, I love how a few embelishments to a very basic cone shape can turn the form into something so instantly recognisable.

These were hollow closed cones with ribbed bodies, a little slip on star and then a Botz green glaze with a bronze luster.

These were also the first ceramics I ever put on sale so have a place in my heart. I only threw ten and all of them vanished quite quickly into new homes.

Functional Sculpture

Pottery and Ceramic isn't just jars and vases, you can have some fun too. I love chickens. They have such unusual features and movements, I've just always enjoyed watching them peck around and see their personalities. Which is why I decided to throw some!

These are exagerated hens, not true to life of course, but caricatures of different personalities and styles, still unmistakeably hens however, thanks to their distinctive Wattles and Comb.

The functunality comes inside. All have been thrown as hollow forms, but some have a secret. If you lift them up they reveal a second thrown piece, and egg-cupped plate! Lovingly kept warm under Mother Hen.

Raku

Raku really feels primal when you watch it. A few glazes on the surface of a piece then gas fired and carbonised in a barrel of combustables. The result when you wipe away the soot is beautiful.

There's a few Raku pieces, including this table bottle vase, in the online store!

Clays

Different clays for different results and glaze effects. I love using this flecked white stoneware as when fired the pigments in the clay really burst out to show speckles in the body.

Sometimes, like this cylinder jar, I'll leave part of the body unglazed to highlight the clay body. It makes it feel a lot more tactile and presents a lovely contrast to the smoothness of the glaze on the top.

Candle Chimneys

Candle Chimneys have accidentally turned into a little range of creations. I love the easy size of these, they can fit in anywhere in the home with minimal effort and are very functional candle holders and perfect tableware.

I've been playing around with various glazes and techniques with these, some are Raku fired which give them a gorgeous earthy smokey crackle. Others are lower fire clays, dipped, then high fired which creates bubbles and textures and some really atmospheric urban industrial finishes.

Size doesn't matter...

I started throwing tinier things as I felt they were getting overlooked with the pursuit of height and size. I love these tiny little cocktail stick holders, both only a few centimetres tall.

Triple glaze dipped and tipped with a dripping metalic rim.
